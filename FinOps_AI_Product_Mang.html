A Practitioner's Memo

The Day FinOps Forced Me
Into the AI Product
Manager Seat

Generative AI cost management isn't a billing problem. It's a product design problem. Here's what changed when I stopped pretending otherwise.

Sumaira Nazir FinOps · AI Product

There was a moment — I can still point to the quarter — when my FinOps practice stopped working. Not failing. Working. Just not enough.

I was managing roughly $1.5M a month in multi-cloud spend across AWS, GCP, and Azure. FOCUS was implemented, anomaly detection was running, the FinOps Champion Program actually had champions in it. And then generative AI workloads started moving from prototype to production, and the questions changed shape under me.

The questions I used to answer were infrastructure questions. Reserved instances. Storage tiers. Egress patterns.

The questions I started getting were product questions:

Why is this prompt 8,000 tokens?

Why did this agent loop for 47 minutes before someone caught it?

Why are we running an LLM-as-judge on every single response?

None of those answers live in a billing console.

§

i.The shift I couldn't avoid

Classical FinOps was built for predictable workloads. EC2 instances behave. S3 buckets behave. BigQuery slots — given a reasonable governance policy — behave. The feedback loop was measured in weeks. Observe. Attribute. Optimize.

Generative AI doesn't wait for the next billing cycle.

A misconfigured agent loop can burn through six figures in a weekend. A poorly designed prompt template, deployed to a million users, becomes a structural cost problem the day it ships. A frontier-model default — chosen because the developer didn't know there was a routing decision to make — quietly turns your gross margin into someone else's API revenue.

These aren't observability problems. They're design problems. And design problems aren't owned by the FinOps team reviewing invoices a month later. They're owned by whoever wrote the spec.

ii.When the hat changed

That's the moment I stopped being only a FinOps practitioner.

I had to start asking questions I'd never had to ask before. What's the unit economics of this feature? Does the cost-per-query still work at 10× the current traffic? At 100×? Where does this feature stop being a viable product?

What's the cheapest model that actually solves this problem? Is this a routing decision, or is everyone defaulting to the most capable tier because no one built the router?

How do I sample evaluations so quality stays high without 3×-ing my inference cost? Where are the agent loop guardrails? What's the human approval gate for high-cost actions during early rollout?

Those aren't FinOps questions. Those are product manager questions.

So I started wearing the hat.

iii.The framework that emerged

Over months of doing this work — partly at Platform.sh, partly through my own multi-agent FinOps project, partly through structured product training — a lifecycle emerged. Not a billing framework. A product framework with cost as a first-class constraint at every stage:

  1. Model selection is a routing decision, not a default. A rules-based router alone can reduce model spend by 60–80%. The frontier tier is gated behind explicit justification, never the unchallenged default.
  2. Prompt design is the cheapest cost lever you have. Compression, caching, and a hard rule that cacheable and dynamic content are tagged separately in the architecture.
  3. Agent architecture needs guardrails before it sees live traffic. Loop limits, token caps per run, budget circuit breakers. Unbounded loops are the number-one source of GenAI billing shock.
  4. Validation gets a sixth axis: cost viability. Estimate cost-per-query, multiply by expected volume, stress-test at 10× and 100× load. Kill features that don't pencil out at scale before infrastructure is provisioned.
  5. Evaluations sample 5–10% of traffic, stratified by query type — not 100%. Hallucination rate gets wired into the FinOps dashboard as a cost event, because re-queries and support tickets have measurable cost.
  6. Execution runs on a 30-day plan. Baseline first. Optimize second. Governance third. Budgets without baselines are guesses dressed up as policy.
  7. Team and ethics anchor everything. Cost ownership belongs in the team building the product, not a central function reviewing invoices a month later. Token efficiency is also a sustainability KPI.

iv.What classical FinOps still gets right

Everything above is calibrated for organizations consuming foundation models via API. That's most of us, most of the time.

For organizations doing custom training — and there are real ones — the older playbook still matters. Spot and preemptible instances for non-critical training jobs, with 70–90% savings if workloads can handle interruption. Model optimization at the architecture level: pruning, quantization, knowledge distillation. Data pipeline efficiency through deduplication, feature selection, sampling. Managed service trade-offs between SageMaker, Vertex AI, and Azure ML.

Classical FinOps isn't wrong. It's just built for a workload class that no longer represents the leading edge of cloud spend.

v.The honest realization

I didn't set out to become an AI product manager. I set out to do my job — manage cloud spend — competently in a world where the workload had changed.

The FinOps Foundation defines the discipline clearly: it enables organizations to maximize the business value of cloud and technology, enable timely data-driven decision-making, and create financial accountability through collaboration between Engineering, Finance, and business teams.

That definition didn't change. But the surface area it covers exploded.

Consider what the Cursor Developer Habits Report (Spring 2026) revealed about the economics of AI-assisted development alone: cost per agent request varies by nearly 9× across model families — from $0.18 to $1.57 for the same workflow, depending on the model behind it. Agent sessions are getting 30% deeper in just two months. Cache-read tokens now dominate roughly 90% of total token activity, making caching architecture a FinOps decision, not just an engineering one. And input context has become nearly 70% of price-equivalent token costs — meaning how much context you feed a model matters more to your bill than what it writes back.

None of those numbers show up in a traditional cloud billing console. Every single one of them is shaped by a product decision — which model to route to, how to design the prompt, whether to cache, how deep to let the agent go.

Maximizing business value now means understanding unit economics at the query level. Timely data-driven decisions means catching a misconfigured agent loop in minutes, not reviewing the bill in weeks. Financial accountability through collaboration means the engineer writing the prompt template, the PM defining the feature, and the FinOps practitioner reviewing the dashboard are all looking at the same cost signal — at design time, not at invoice time.

You cannot optimize what you didn't help design. You cannot govern what you don't own. And you cannot own a GenAI cost problem if you don't understand the product decisions that created it.

The hat change wasn't a career pivot. It was a job requirement. The FinOps definition always implied cross-functional collaboration. Generative AI just made it impossible to practice without it.

§

The next generation of FinOps practitioners will be part-time product managers. The next generation of AI product managers will be part-time FinOps practitioners. The line between the two is already gone — most organizations just haven't noticed yet.

The question isn't whether to invest in AI. That decision is made.

The question is whether the people governing the spend are sitting close enough to the people designing the product to catch the problem at the moment it's created — not at the moment the invoice arrives.
